The late 1990's work of David Dunning an Justin Kruger is often oversimplified into some form of "Stupid people are too stupid to know they are stupid." And early-stage false confidence described in the effect does somewhat validate that point, though not as a constant (ie "Stupid people") but more as a stage in an evolution (ie "People who do not know enough yet"). But the Dunning-Kruger effect is in no way monopolized by the unintelligent masses. In fact, if you are a person that habitually endeavors to master new things (and especially if you have a formidable base of general intelligence) then you have undoubtedly either come to identify and account for the effect, or suffered repeatedly from it.



### A Micro-Primer in Dunning-Kruger

> [!NOTE]
> 
> This is a hyper-synopsis and focused purely on my own experience for the sake of this post. Please see [David Dunning's paper](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/chapter/bookseries/abs/pii/B9780123855220000056?via%3Dihub) to form your own opinions in full. 



Complex skills exist within "horizons of ignorance." We start out with only our general intelligence (stuff we know, not specifically related to the skill) and a limited idea of what we don't know about the skill - this is our horizon. As we learn about the skill we learn not only the skill basics, but a growing breadth of what we don't know - our horizon expands. This is Dunning-Kruger.



### What About The First Horizon (Year One)?

I wish I had the forethought to capture that first learning horizon in 2024. There is plenty of evidence indicating how little I understood sailing and living aboard - my written plans, the challenges I ran into, and eventually the end of my first boat -  but hindsight makes it impossible to really comprehend the hubris that comes with knowing very little. I purchased an old boat with the sum total experience of a weekend sailing course under my belt. I went out into the bay several times by myself without checking weather or conditions, my very first solo sail during a small craft advisory. I sailed up the Chesapeake to Philly with help, then back down the bay by myself - repeatedly making panic mistakes ("survival reactions" in Keith Code parlance) when the sea state was ugly or the wind picked up. I replaced and then destroyed parts of the craft, stumbling south, until finally the ship was completely disabled. That year ended with a Coast Guard tow into port. There was so much learned, and more importantly, the first horizon of what I needed to know (and didn't) took shape.
