When most people mention Dunning-Kruger they reference a shorthand interpretation that "Stupid people are too stupid to know they aren't smart, and smart people are so smart they think they are stupid." This is most often used to explain the mass appeal of charismatic ignorance in politics, science, medicine etc. 

But crowd impact is a minor and mostly bastardized footnote from the [pioneering research of David Dunning and Justin Kruger almost 30 years ago](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/chapter/bookseries/abs/pii/B9780123855220000056?via%3Dihub), one that fuels distain and distracts from the far more interesting, and important, central elements of the study. The core of the Dunning-Kruger effect is really about personal "horizons of ignorance" on a given subject - a pairing of knowledge/experience with an awareness of what one _does not yet know_. Dunning-Kruger describes the evolution of individual human expertise, and it gives us a mechanically sound map of our own hubris. 

Illustrated another way: think of any skill or subject you wish to master as a far-away mountain you have decided to climb. At the onset of the journey the climb appears easy and you are very confident - the mountain looks small and green and inviting, far off in the distance. As you trek closer to the mountain, more of the challenges become visible; deadly sheer rock faces, broken trails, dark forests. You begin to climb and encounter tracks of mountain lions, evidence of landslides, and even tiny crosses marking the final resting place of defeated climbers before you. Standing half way up the mountain trail you are far more knowledgeable than when you started, and far less confident in your success. You think of the lions and rockslides you were blissfully unaware of at the start, and realize how many _more_ things you likely are still aware of now. That is Dunning-Kruger. 

## Horizons of Ignorance

I find most adults stay comfortably inside their first "sphere of ignorance." Their hobbies and interests are hereditary, formed during childhood and cemented in young adulthood as a permanent identity. Most adults love to fish because they have always fished. They are passionate about the sports they grew up with (first playing, now watching and now living vicariously through kids). They play music, or dance, or ride motorcycles because they were able to couple learning a skill with the general learning of childhood, and consequently never suffer the embarrassment of _not being good at something_. ### Power and Sail Are Very Different Cultures

{{< figure src="dunno.png" caption="dunno really, maybe drunk solomans" >}}

This is a gross generalization that will likely piss off a lot of people I know, but it is a generalization I find accurate enough that I wish I learned it sooner. People participate in sports/pastimes for very different reasons. I started riding motorcycles 25 years ago, and initially I would ride out with anyone on two wheels; it didn't take me long to realize that what I wanted from my ride - adrenaline, skill mastery, continual improvement as an athlete - was completely incompatible with my Harley riding friends. They loved the pageantry of dressing up, the performative highway theater of throttle-snarling, and the slow-roll through city traffic like a marching band in a parade. Nothing wrong with that, just not my scene. My rides searched out deserted backroads, elevation changes, and challenging turns; their rides mapped out the most populated route from one happy hour to the next. There was very little advice we could share, because we wanted very different things from our ride time.

On *Defiant* I value independence and self-sufficiency. Sails are slow but wind is free, unregulated, uncontrolled. A solar + turbine + lithium system requires a tremendous amount of care and feeding to support the power needs of everyday life, and even then it cannot accommodate the electro-gluttony of urban existence. I moved aboard so I could skirt a system I have increasingly little faith in, and so whatever the future holds I am able to meet that future on my own terms.

Powerboat owners are entirely reliant on that system by design. Their boats require enormous amounts of fuel, fuel that must be drilled, shipped, refined, shipped again, and sold at a price based on today's news headlines. All but the very largest power yachts are incapable of making passage in rough seas. So powerboats require thousands of dollars a day to move, and cannot go very far when they do. This translates into two types of powerboat owners: floating beach houses and toys.

The beach house crowd relies on an array of umbilical connections to the system - usually 2+ shore power connections and a plumbed freshwater line - to make life aboard indistinguishable from that on land. They take 20 minute showers. Every inch of the boat is a constant 70°F inside. They have full-size refrigerators they purchased at Home Depot. On the rare occasion they do unplug to head out for a few hours, it is as if the boat is wearing a space suit and must quickly complete a mission and then scamper home before the oxygen reserves run out.

The toy crowd is even more like the Harley crowd; they are usually loud, often drunk, and most of their vessel is for show. They may have a queen bed in each cabin, but they are going to sleep in a hotel anyway. They have never used the head. Good days on the water are glass-calm between tiki bars. Everything can be solved by burning more dinosaurs.

I am not knocking these people - I am happy they found something they enjoy and are able to do it. But there is not much crossover between my life and theirs where boats are concerned, and that matters. It matters when I ask if a port is a good place to stop, and to me "good place" means easy to get provisions, stuff to do, good anchor holding. To a powerboat owner "good place" almost always means cool bar and not a lot of BUI police checkpoints. When a powerboat goes somewhere, it is a form of vacation or recreation - because normal life is in the slip or land house. My ship is always "going somewhere," and so a "cool bar" is far less interesting than a clean wash-and-fold laundromat. If you look at the transoms outside any dockside tiki bar, you will find the hailing ports of most powerboats are no more than twenty minutes away from the town you are in. The sailboats will be from all over the world. That's not better or worse, but the lives are entirely incompatible in many ways.

### Sea State And Wind Are Not Coupled

_At least not the way I thought they were_

When you take basic sailing courses they drill into you how important wind intensity is, how you need to reef early and reef often, how the wind creates waves in cases where the current and the wind oppose, wind wind wind wind.... but they never talk about sea state. In fact, in two years of learning anything I could from anyone (including paid training, ASA courses, and lots and lots of time at tiki bars) I can't remember anyone ever saying a single word about wave height, or period, or wave direction. A 6ft swell with a 1 second period will rip you apart like a table saw, but nobody ever said one damn word about it. 

Coming from powersports, and not persuaded otherwise, I developed a mental model that sea state was basically like road traction. Sailboats could be out in the nasty, I reasoned, because the lift force of the keel + sail stabilizes the boat - kind of like cranking up the throttle to keep a tire hooked up in a turn. This was a tiny bit right (yes the lift from the main + keel helps stabilize a bit) and a whole lot wrong (more power does not make you more stable, it just overpowers the sails and makes you shit your pants and/or die). 

In reality, sea state is a lot more like road noise - it is slightly related to the speed/power delivery of wind + sails, but it is _mostly_ independent of them and impacts you independently as well. If you drop your sails, big ugly waves don't go away.  How hard you charge them has little impact on how hard they hit you. You can point the boat into them instead of taking them on the beam, or you can tuff it out if they are traveling in a direction parallel to yours, these are most often decisions around comfort and fatigue than they are purely speed and stability concerns. 

{{< figure src="keel.png" caption="That is a Dodge Ram with a bed full of cinderblocks hanging down there" >}}

Sailboats can _survive_ out in the nasty because they have a 3 ton lead set of truck nuts hanging 5+ feet below them, and it takes godlike force to displace that much metal for even a few seconds. This doesn't mean being inside that boat is comfortable for all that  tossing, it just means that if you manage not to die and stay inside during the ugly bits, you will have arrived in one piece. There is no way to trim the sails that will meaningfully change the effect of sea state on the boat - they are two different systems that only work together. 

### Sailboats Are Sometimes Submarines

I didn't get all the companionway covers and sealing port lights, dodgers and dorade boxes. Now I do. When the sea picks up enough, _the whole damn boat_ goes underwater. Maybe forward crashing waves break over the bow, or terrifying waves on the beam flood the gunnels and the cockpit. Maybe you are sailing faster than hull speed and getting sucked down in beats. But there are absolutely times where any given part of the boat can, and will, be submerged in water.  Some kinds of sailing are less "sitting on top of the water" and more "basically submerging and bobbing back to the surface undead."

### Motoring Is A Central Part Of Sailboat Life

Two factors in my life exacerbate the need to motor: I mostly single hand, and I have a full-time day job. How are these related? 

If you are brave enough, and committed enough to fossil-free sailing, you can make it anywhere without burning dinos. Eventually. 

let's say you have a little wind coming directly from your destination 40nm away. You are "beating up", zig-zagging toward the destination at 2.5kts. At this pace, you will need to tack every 2 hours or so, and you will reach that destination in about 4 days (because you're only making a mile or so up for every 5 miles you travel). If you have crew you can take turns sleeping and keeping watch, but if you are single handed that will mean staying awake for 4 days straight, no naps. Of course the weather won't stay put for 4 days, so maybe you'll get a heavy blow from the other direction tomorrow and be pushed there in 2 days! Or, maybe the sea state will turn and you'll find yourself struggling with brutal conditions after not sleeping for 31 hours. 

Now let's say you are me, and you are single handing and have to be at work tomorrow. Regardless of what the weather looks like at 2am, I can't sit out here and wait - I need to be at anchor somewhere and asleep. You may think I should be able to set the autopilot and go to bed, like a modern-day Joshua Slocum entrusting the fate of his ship to the ghosts of Christopher Columbus' crew; Slocum had many perils to face, but city-sized freighters traveling at 30kts were not among his worries. No, you fire up the motor, furl the headsail, and head dead-upwind at 4kts as the crow flies to the destination. It is still 10 hours of motoring at this pace; maybe the wind shifts a few hours from now, and I can haul out that jib and that staysail and make 5kts in. Maybe the sea state suddenly picks up, and I am very glad I didn't sit and wait out there. Regardless, the sun is above the horizon when I finally set my anchor. 

Motoring isn't cheating, it is an alternative to not going at all. The motor replaces crew, and time, I don't have and would need to otherwise make the journey. 

### 'Everything Is Broken' Is A Lifestyle

I still haven't quite learned this completely. On the dock everything is easier to fix, especially with a truck parked right in front of the slip to go run for parts. So broken things become a list, and that list becomes the reason you can't leave. To be fair, there is a fuzzy line between the initial refit and just "broken shit life" and I'm still learning to navigate that. But part of living on a 40 year old ship, especially one that's seen some serious neglect, is the art of holding her together underway. There is a romance embodied by the great sci-fi space mechanics - Chewbacca, Miles O'Brian, Kaylee Frye among others. When I start to think about heat shrink butt connectors as "power couplings" part of me is living out a preteen fantasy. 

{{< figure src="mechanics.png" caption="maybe try routing auxiliary power through the phase inverter?" >}}

I think the distinction between remaining refit and perpetual fixing is four parts (at least that's my theory at the moment): 

* **Can _Defiant_ move without it?** 
  If the ship can't move (eg broken v-belt) then wherever we are, we stop and fix it now.
- **Has it worked before? **
  If it has worked before and is broken again (eg the Electro scan) we need to be able to fix it along the way, cast off.

- **Is it possible to fix underway?**
  Do we need specific parts we don't have, special tools, skills that need to be outsourced? Solve those things. Then do it underway.

- **How badly do I need to leave?**
  At some point, even limping to the next port and fixing things there is better than overstaying a welcome. 

### The "Promise Land" Sucks

When it comes to vendors, suppliers, contractors, and generally anyone doing business in the marine industry as a whole, I expect them not to keep their word. This is a terrible thing to have to say, but my default has slowly moved to "bullshit" until proven otherwise. If a contractor says he will be by on Tuesday, I may see him Thursday - and if there is a problem with his work I will never see him again. If a sail maker promises me that stitching will be done by the end of the month, I am no longer shocked to see my sail untouched and exactly in the spot I dropped it off 7 weeks later.  I have worked in all types of industries, from building construction to healthcare, education to finance, and I have never seen an entire system function in such a bubble of mutual disrespect as this one. It isn't just that people in the space are consistently unreliable, it's that there is no consequence for that unreliability - a welder does a shit job and then refuses to fix his work, and somehow the same client goes back to him with the next job. 

A friend referred to this as "the promise land" because everyone promises everything, and then does nothing. I have to say I agree, and I do my best to plan accordingly. 

### Condo Eco Hawks Are Petulant Children

Living aboard has given me a deep understanding of the energy production, consumption, and storage required for daily life. I think of watts much the same way I can contextualize degrees Fahrenheit - I know that 70℉ is comfortable and 90℉ is hot,  the 800W burst of my kettle for just a minute is only ~13Wh, where my macbook's constant suck is 30Wh+ all day long.
