When _Defiant_ [finally crossed the finish line](/blog/motor-motors-sails-sail) two weeks ago I expected to be elated. This was the first, most monumental event in the long saga of her refit, not to mention my first big moment of triumph claiming her back from nearly half a century of decay. But there was no excitement or satisfaction, was just _tired_. The weight of a year's work settled like lead in my shoulders. After I had tied off at the dock, I made a half-hearted attempt to tune my dock lines; the boat might as well have been a mountain range for all the effect my efforts had on adjusting her. The weather forecast for the next week was a solid wall of rain, and it seemed like the universe was sending me a crystal-clear message: _take a break_. So I took off north to visit friends and family almost immediately.

When I returned a week later everything was still in disarray. Lines were heaped in tangled masses in the cockpit. I had left the instrument panels on. The vessel management system was non-responsive. Oh and the dinghy was a little bit off. 

{{< figure src="dinghy.png" caption="I don't think it's supposed to hang that way" >}}

### Batteries

The VMS was down because power had failed. This was to be the beginning of a hands-on education into discount LifePO4 batteries, but for now I had plenty of voltage showing and the system should have been back up. So restart scripts don't work, good to know. 

Six of the batteries were now fully charged. Three were at under five percent and in alarm state (meaning they were refusing to take more power). I'll do a more in-depth writeup on LifePO4 nonsense later; the short short version I discovered after much pain was that the batteries had to be removed from the system, gently leveled out of panic mode over several days of trickle charging, then _slowly_ recharged at full voltage, and finally could then be re-introduced to the battery bank.  So that was three days of nonsense. 

But why had the batteries died in the first place? True I left a few things on, but _Defiant was connected to shore power_. There was also nearly 1Kw of solar and a wind generator - more than enough juice to handle the standing loads in most conditions. So what happened?  

That week of rain also meant overcast skies and nearly zero solar production. Shore power was cut off when a neighbor's poorly-secured power cable fell into the water, shorting our shared pedestal breaker. So the batteries were left to drain :crying_cat_face:.

### Dinghy Damage

The week's rain was also to blame for my broken dinghy transom - or more accurately, the rain combined with carelessness on my part. One of the many things I had forgotten to do before leaving was pull the drain plug in the dinghy; so the hanging vessel filled with rain, became too heavy, and :boom: the fiberglass hook ripped in half. Since I had three down days while the batteries charged anyway, this seemed like an opportunity to beef up the little tender. 

{{< figure src="snapped_dinghy.png" caption="We can rebuild it. We have the technology." >}}

I really like this little inflatable; most people seem to prefer bigger hard-bottom dinghies, but to be honest I still don't see why. Maybe that will change as I use the tender for more complicated things, or if more crew than just Bash and I need to shuttle around? But for now I am perfectly content with this one. However the transom kinda sucks, it is basically painted plywood. So my first step was to drill out all the self-tapping screw holes and fill them with epoxy, making it both ugly and super strong. 

{{< figure src="drill_and_fill.png" caption="Epoxy fixes everything" >}}

I drilled out the new epoxy plugs and through-bolted a pair of stainless steel hangers. With the plate re-mounted, the dinghy was back in action.
