The last three weeks have been less of a planned campaign and more of a sprint through the things that have to happen before _Defiant_ is ready to leave Windmill Marina. Nothing individually dramatic — mostly a pile of items that had been sitting on the list long enough.

### Sails

The biggest project was getting the staysail into something resembling a working sail. It had been living in a bag, and as I once captured with great elegance in my project notes: "the bag sucks." Correct.

Getting the furler installed meant dropping the staysail stay and cutting off the swage fitting at the head — an angle grinder was involved. Before the sail could even enter the furler drum, the bolt rope had to be resewn. The existing bolt was a quarter-inch and the furler drum wants five-sixteenths, so that was an afternoon of hand-sewing to get a proper fit. Once that was sorted I sewed a UV sacrificial cover for when the sail is furled out and a full cover for when it's off the stay entirely. The staysail now has more outerwear than I do.

While the rigging was already in chaos, I replaced the jib furling line and re-lashed the bimini solar panels so they'd stop working loose every time we get some wind.

### Engine

The engine bay is now lined with Soundown insulation. Running the diesel is roughly 40% less like standing next to a running generator in a phone booth. With everything apart, I also swapped out the fuel sender — the old one had stopped giving any useful reading — and pulled out a zip tie that had been moonlighting as a transmission mount clip for longer than I care to admit.

### Plumbing

The salt pump on the head gave up, which it managed to do as inconveniently as possible. New pump in, working as intended. The drinking tap now has a proper 3-stage filter, and the main water line has a UV filter upstream of everything else. Whatever was living in the dock water stays on the dock now.

### Electrical

Three of the house batteries had gone flat while I was away and dropped into alarm state — meaning they were refusing to accept charge. Getting them back meant disconnecting them from the bank and running a very slow trickle charge for a few days to ease them out of panic mode before they'd accept a full charge again. All three came back. Full bank.

### Inspection

The Coast Guard came by. We passed.

With the staysail furler rigged, the batteries healthy, and the boxes mostly checked, the boat is starting to feel like she might actually leave the dock sometime soon.
