We all have some casual escape fantasy, one our psyche uses as a pressure release valve on reality. I would guess that shipping out on an old sailboat is a common "fuck it all" daydream; the weight of constant connection, the breakneck pace, and the overwhelming material excess of our everyday lives demand an escape that is polar opposite - slow, simple, solitary. Industries are built on a slow-drip indulgence of this fantasy - boatyards full of "someday" vessels decaying on the hard, their owners paying yard fees to keep the dream on life support - but unable to reconcile the dream with reality. 



Two years into this endeavor I want, maybe need, to understand why things went differently in my case, and what keeps me on this path now. It is dangerously easy to conflate a concrete goal with a sense of self-purpose, and in doing so allow that goal to consume everything else - Ahab's proverbial white whale. I have to ask myself now: is _Defiant_ still a course headed to where I want to go, or has she become my _Moby Dick_? The further we deviate from a beaten path in life, the more important it becomes to de-insulate our story with brutal honesty - lest we find ourselves destroyed by our best ambitions. 



In April '24 I did not give much thought to _why_ I was suddenly dead-set on traveling the western world aboard a sailboat. This wasn't a life-long dream - in fact sailing was not a part of my life in any way at the time. I had crewed on a little J27 sloop exactly two times during my ASA 101 sailing class, an experience which has as much in common with live aboard cruising as it does with competitive hot dog eating. Nevertheless, it became my mission. I researched. I planned. I created a ten month timeline, budgeted and set milestones. By that July I had finished the condo renovation and found tenants, sold nearly everything I owned, found a boat almost six hours away, and moved onboard.

On the surface were pragmatic arguments. I had always wanted to be able to travel and snowbird during winters (I despise cold weather). Had any of the startups I was involved with over the years (including my own) ponied up the ever-elusive "big exit," I would have done just that - purchased an apartment in Miami or San Juan, kept one in Philly or New York, and traveled between the two. That was no longer a financial reality; but with a boat, I could theoretically snowbird on a fraction of the budget - no mortgages, HOA fees or flights. I wouldn't even have to pack a bag. Finances, in general, were a big motivator. I was (still am) the quintessential 21st century HENRY - in the top 4% individual income bracket in the United States, yet unable to establish any type of long-term financial independence. I was hand-to-mouth at scale. Spending \$20k on an old boat and then "living for free" on anchor could act as a lever to finally fix that ratio. A lower fixed cashflow requirement would mean I could take on less work. Less work would free up time for me to do more for myself (like cook and clean) instead of outsourcing. Less outsourcing would free up more cashflow - and the cycle continues. 



But let's be honest - if this was just about fixing the books, there were easier (and likely more effective) alternatives to moving onto a sailboat. I could have rented out my condo, and moved into a cheap apartment in a less affluent neighborhood. I could have taken similar austerity measures, reducing my work hours so I could take on more at home. Moving onboard was, above all, about identity.
