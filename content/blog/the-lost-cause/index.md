We all have some casual escape fantasy, one our psyche uses as a pressure release valve on reality. I would guess that shipping out on an old sailboat is a common "fuck it all" daydream; the weight of constant connection, the breakneck pace, and the overwhelming material excess of our everyday lives demand an escape that is polar opposite - slow, simple, solitary. Industries are built on a slow-drip indulgence of this fantasy - boatyards full of "someday" vessels decaying on the hard, their owners paying yard fees to keep the dream on life support - but unable to reconcile the dream with reality. 

Two years into this endeavor I want, maybe need, to understand why things went differently in my case, and what keeps me on this path now. It is dangerously easy to conflate a concrete goal with a sense of self-purpose, and in doing so allow that goal to consume everything else - Ahab's proverbial white whale. I have to ask myself now: is _Defiant_ still a course headed to where I want to go, or has she become my _Moby Dick_? The further we deviate from a beaten path in life, the more important it becomes to de-insulate our story with brutal honesty - lest we find ourselves destroyed by our best ambitions. 

In April '24, my life had come to a sort of crossroads. After two years my startup had shut down; the venture that followed was short and ugly. My relationship had come to a bitter end. Philly's social climate had changed, and I suddenly felt unwanted in my own city. While this certainly wasn't the first time I found myself starting over, it was the first time I could not find the faith to do so. 

I have been a HENRY my entire working life. I am in the top 4% income earners in the country, yet am perpetually unable to break through into financial independence. Existing this way requires you to believe in a trajectory, to trust that the system that will process your many sacrifices and output prosperity. In my 20's that faith was in the US housing market, which earned me first my highest (unadjusted!) lifetime net worth, and then a bankruptcy shortly after. My 30's revolved around startup equity and the ever-elusive "big exit" - which eluded me all the way to my early 40's. At 41 I doubled down, founding a tech startup myself; two more years of hundred-hour weeks like so many logs on the fire before that ran out of runway. 

Throughout it all, I held _sacrifice_ sacred as the key to inevitable success. Insane hours, ravenous ambition, neglect of my family and relationships until they fell apart. I quit drinking at 25 because I was worried I might miss an off-hours deal. I commuted four hours to work to arrive two hours early and learn code written in a language I didn't know. I spent my weekends working, getting my master's degree, building my own startup, while my friends were at football games and raising children. Time and again I had lain offerings at the alter of financial independence; but the Gods of hustle culture still refused to make it rain. In April '24, my well of faith had finally run dry. Reality had collapsed on the twenty-year myth - that I was following the redemption arc of my own hero's journey.

did not give much thought to _why_ I was suddenly dead-set on traveling the western world aboard a sailboat. This wasn't a life-long dream - in fact sailing was not a part of my life in any way at the time. I had crewed on a little J27 sloop exactly two times during my ASA 101 sailing class, an experience which has as much in common with live aboard cruising as it does with competitive hot dog eating. 





I did understand what I was trying to solve for - broken things that needed an atomic-bomb sized fix if my life was going to change direction. 



Nevertheless, it became my mission. I researched. I planned. I created a ten month timeline, budgeted and set milestones. By that July I had finished the condo renovation and found tenants, sold nearly everything I owned, found a boat almost six hours away, and moved onboard.
